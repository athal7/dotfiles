# Ladder of Inference

**Category:** Decision Making
**Source:** Chris Argyris (Harvard); popularized by Peter Senge in *The Fifth Discipline*

Use this when you're about to act on a strong conclusion, but the reasoning that got you there may have skipped steps. Also useful for challenging others' conclusions in a structured, non-confrontational way.

## The Seven Rungs (bottom to top)

```
7. Actions        ← what we do
6. Beliefs        ← what we hold to be true
5. Conclusions    ← what we decide
4. Assumptions    ← what we take for granted
3. Interpretations ← how we give meaning to data
2. Selected data  ← what we pay attention to
1. Available data ← observable reality
```

We climb this ladder unconsciously and very fast. The danger is skipping rungs — jumping from "I selected this data" directly to "I'm taking this action" without noticing what assumptions were made in between.

## How to Apply

When you catch yourself (or someone else) at a high rung with uncertain footing, **work down the ladder** using these questions:

| Rung | Guiding question |
|------|-----------------|
| Actions | Why do I think this is the right action? What alternatives exist? |
| Beliefs | What belief am I acting from? What conclusions underlie it? |
| Conclusions | Why did I conclude this? What assumptions led here? |
| Assumptions | Are these assumptions valid? What am I taking for granted? |
| Interpretations | Am I reading this data objectively? What other meanings could it have? |
| Selected data | What did I ignore? What sources haven't I looked at? |

Once at the bottom, rebuild your reasoning **consciously** back up.

## Agent Workflow

When a conclusion or decision seems to have been reached too quickly:

1. Identify what **action or belief** is on the table.
2. Walk down the ladder, making each rung explicit.
3. Flag which rungs are weak — missing data, questionable assumptions, or only one interpretation considered.
4. Suggest what additional data or reframing would strengthen (or overturn) the conclusion.
5. Rebuild a more grounded version of the reasoning.

## Example

**Situation:** "This developer is underperforming. I should let them go."

Working down:
- **Action:** Fire the developer
- **Belief:** They're not cut out for the job
- **Conclusion:** They're a bad developer
- **Assumption:** Missed deadlines = inefficiency = poor skill
- **Interpretation:** Missed deadlines mean the person is the problem
- **Selected data:** Only looked at deadlines, not at ticket complexity or team context
- **Available data:** Also available: team workload, deadline realism, personal circumstances, code quality metrics

**Weak rungs:** Interpretation (one possible reading of missed deadlines) and Selected data (only deadline data consulted).

**Better action:** Have a direct conversation before concluding anything. Check whether deadlines are realistic and what the actual blockers are.
