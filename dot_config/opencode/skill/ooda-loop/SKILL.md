# OODA Loop

**Category:** Decision Making
**Source:** Colonel John Boyd, US Air Force

Use this in fast-moving, competitive, or ambiguous situations where you can't wait for complete information. The goal is not just to make good decisions — it's to cycle faster than your competition.

## The Four Phases

### 1. Observe
Gather available information quickly. This includes:
- Current situation and context
- External signals (market, users, competitors, incidents)
- Feedback from the previous loop iteration

**Warning:** Data becomes stale. Observation that takes too long may produce decisions based on outdated reality.

### 2. Orient
Synthesize what you observed. This is the most important phase — it's where mental models, experience, and analysis come together to understand what's actually happening.

- Challenge your assumptions
- Consider multiple interpretations
- Factor in prior knowledge and others' perspectives
- Identify what you don't know

Poor orientation leads to acting on a misunderstood situation even with good data.

### 3. Decide
Choose a course of action — a hypothesis to test. This is not about certainty; it's about making a time-bounded, informed bet.

Balance speed and thoroughness. A decision made too slowly is often worse than an imperfect decision made quickly.

### 4. Act
Implement the decision. This tests your observation and orientation — you'll quickly learn what was accurate and what wasn't.

The output of Act feeds back into the next Observe phase.

## Tempo Matters

Going through the loop once is not the goal. **Cycling faster than your competition** is. An organization that completes more OODA loops in a given time period gains increasing advantage — each loop improves orientation, which improves subsequent decisions.

## Agent Workflow

When helping navigate a fast-moving situation:

1. **Observe:** What information is currently available? What just happened?
2. **Orient:** What does this mean? What are the plausible interpretations? What's the most likely reality?
3. **Decide:** What should be done right now? State it as a hypothesis.
4. **Act:** What specific action implements this decision? Who does what?
5. **Next loop:** What will we observe to validate or invalidate this decision?

## Example

**Situation:** A competitor just released a feature that's generating significant buzz.

**Observe:** Monitor social media sentiment, read competitor release notes, check support tickets for user mentions, look at product analytics for related feature usage.

**Orient:** The feature addresses a real gap in the market. Our users have asked for something similar. The competitor's implementation has a known limitation (requires manual setup). Our team has adjacent infrastructure already built.

**Decide:** Ship a version of this feature in 3 weeks with automated setup as our differentiator. Don't try to match all their capabilities — lead with the automated angle.

**Act:** Assign one team to scoping, another to prep the announcement. Check in daily. Re-observe user reaction within 48 hours of launch.

## When Not to Use OODA

OODA is optimized for speed and adaptability. For high-stakes, irreversible decisions, slow down and use Second-Order Thinking or Decision Matrix instead. OODA is best for decisions that are **reversible or testable**.

## Related Tools

- **Second-Order Thinking** — when the decision is high-stakes and needs deeper consequence analysis
- **Hard Choice Model** — to calibrate how much deliberation the decision deserves
- **Cynefin Framework** — to understand whether the situation calls for OODA-style experimentation or expert analysis
