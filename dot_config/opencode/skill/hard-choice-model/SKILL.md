# Hard Choice Model

**Category:** Decision Making
**Source:** Wes O'Haire (Dropbox)

Use this before investing effort in a decision. Many people over-deliberate easy decisions and under-deliberate hard ones. This model helps you calibrate.

## The 2×2 Matrix

|  | **Easy to Compare** | **Hard to Compare** |
|--|--------------------|--------------------|
| **High Impact** | **Big Choice** — take your time, gather info | **Hard Choice** — accept uncertainty, use frameworks |
| **Low Impact** | **No-Brainer** — decide quickly | **Apples & Oranges** — decide by current priorities |

## The Four Types

### 1. No-Brainer (Low impact + Easy to compare)
Simple decisions, similar options, low stakes. The cost of deliberating exceeds the cost of a suboptimal choice.

**Approach:** Decide immediately. Optimizing is a waste of time.

*Examples: Which day to schedule a meeting. Which color for a button. Which linter rule to enable.*

### 2. Apples & Oranges (Low impact + Hard to compare)
Options are different in kind, but stakes are still low.

**Approach:** Decide based on current priorities. Don't build a framework for it.

*Examples: Reading vs. exercising tonight. Conference vs. online course.*

### 3. Big Choice (High impact + Easy to compare)
Important decision, but options are comparable on the same dimensions.

**Approach:** Take time to gather information. Build confidence through thorough comparison. Use a Decision Matrix.

*Examples: Choosing between two job offers with similar roles. Picking between two vendors on cost/feature/support.*

### 4. Hard Choice (High impact + Hard to compare)
The genuinely difficult decisions. Major impact, but options are different in kind — you can't just score them.

**Approach:** Accept there's no objectively right answer. Evaluate different factors explicitly (Decision Matrix can help). Run experiments to reduce uncertainty if possible. Don't wait for perfect information.

*Examples: Stay IC or move into management. Join a startup or a big company. Rebuild vs. refactor.*

## Agent Workflow

When someone presents a decision:

1. Ask (or assess): How significant is the outcome?
2. Ask (or assess): How easy is it to compare the options?
3. Place the decision in the quadrant.
4. Name the decision type and recommend the appropriate approach.
5. If it's a Hard Choice, suggest Decision Matrix + experiments. If it's a No-Brainer, say so and recommend deciding immediately.

## Common Mistake

Spending hours on No-Brainers and Apples & Oranges while underinvesting in Big Choices and Hard Choices. The model's value is surfacing this mismatch.

## Related Tools

- **Decision Matrix** — for Big Choices and Hard Choices where explicit scoring helps
- **Eisenhower Matrix** — if the decision is about *tasks* rather than *options*
- **OODA Loop** — if the decision needs to be made fast despite incomplete information
