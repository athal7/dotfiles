# Second-Order Thinking

**Category:** Decision Making
**Source:** Howard Marks, Shane Parrish

Use this when a decision seems straightforward but might have unintended downstream effects. Most people stop at first-order thinking; second-order thinking is what separates good decisions from great ones.

## Core Concept

- **First-order thinking:** What is the immediate effect of this decision?
- **Second-order thinking:** What happens as a result of that effect? And then what?

Effects compound. A decision that looks good in the short term may be damaging long-term, and vice versa.

## How to Apply

### Method 1: "And then what?"
1. State the decision.
2. List the immediate first-order effects.
3. For each effect, ask: **"And then what?"**
4. Repeat 2–3 times to trace the chain of consequences.

### Method 2: 10/10/10 Timelines
Ask: What are the consequences of this decision in...
- **10 minutes?** (immediate)
- **10 months?** (medium-term)
- **10 years?** (long-term)

## Agent Workflow

When asked to apply second-order thinking to a decision:

1. **State the decision** clearly.
2. **First-order effects:** list 3–5 immediate consequences.
3. **Second-order effects:** for each first-order effect, trace "and then what?" 1–2 levels deeper.
4. **Highlight surprises:** which second-order effects are non-obvious or counter-intuitive?
5. **Recommendation:** does this analysis change the initial assessment of the decision?

## Example

**Decision:** Offer a generous free tier to grow user acquisition.

| Order | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| 1st | More users sign up |
| 2nd | Support load increases; infrastructure costs grow |
| 3rd | Engineering team is diverted to support; burn rate accelerates |
| 1st | Brand awareness grows |
| 2nd | Enterprise prospects see free users as social proof |
| 3rd | Conversion from free to paid improves — or free users entrench and resist paying |

**Non-obvious risk:** If the free tier is too generous, it trains users to expect everything for free, making conversion harder long-term. This would not be visible from first-order analysis alone.

## Common Traps

- **Assuming effects are linear** — they usually aren't; they compound
- **Stopping too early** — one level of "and then what?" is rarely enough
- **Only tracing negative chains** — also trace positive second-order effects to find hidden upside

## Related Tools

- **Inversion** — pair with second-order thinking to find worst-case chains
- **Iceberg Model** — for understanding systemic, hidden causes (vs. downstream effects)
- **Balancing/Reinforcing Feedback Loops** — second-order effects often involve feedback dynamics
